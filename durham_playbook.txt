Durham First Multifamily Deal Package
Playbook • neighborhood analysis • screening system • scorecard guide
Built for your first small/midsize acquisition in Durham


A quick note before the playbook
You do not need a heroic first deal. You need a disciplined first deal that is understandable, financeable, and fixable. That is the standard this package is built around.
1. How to use this package
• Read the playbook once end-to-end before looking at a live deal.
• Use the workbook to lock your buy box, compare neighborhoods, and score live opportunities.
• Screen fast. Underwrite deeply only when a deal survives the screen.
• If a deal requires you to talk yourself into it, it is probably not your first deal.
2. Durham market frame
• Durham’s average home-value proxy is about $390,598 and average asking rent proxy is about $1,629.
• The broader Raleigh-Durham multifamily market had unusually strong renter absorption through 2024 and 2025, even while supply growth weighed on near-term fundamentals.
• That backdrop supports demand, but it does not remove execution risk. Your edge has to come from basis discipline, neighborhood choice, and operational clarity.
What that means for you: buy something that can work even if rent growth is ordinary. Let the location help you, but do not depend on the market to bail out weak underwriting.
3. First-deal acquisition playbook
Step 1 — Freeze the buy box
• Target 6–20 units for the first deal unless an unusually strong opportunity justifies stretching.
• Stay in B-/C+ territory with light-to-moderate value-add only.
• Avoid heavy construction, severe tenant distress, and bookkeeping chaos.
Step 2 — Narrow the map
• Do not cover all of Durham at once.
• Pick 3–4 neighborhoods from this package and become opinionated about them.
• You are trying to know a few blocks well, not the whole city vaguely.
Step 3 — Build a small local team
• You need 1–2 brokers, a lender, a real estate attorney, a property manager, an inspector/GC, and an insurance broker.
• For deal one, team quality matters more than squeezing the last 50 bps of economics.
Step 4 — Screen hard
• Kill deals early if records are sloppy, taxes/insurance are fantasy, or the value-add story is vague.
• A good first deal should look boringly workable.
Step 5 — Underwrite conservatively
• Use real reserves, real repairs, realistic bad debt, and realistic turnover pace.
• The question is not 'Can I make this work?' It is 'Does this still work if things go merely okay?'
Step 6 — Diligence like an operator
• Physical diligence: roof, plumbing, electrical, drainage, sewer, HVAC, deferred maintenance.
• Financial diligence: T-12, rent roll, collections, concessions, capex, payroll, taxes, insurance.
• Legal diligence: title, survey, permits, zoning, code, liens, claims.
Step 7 — Negotiate for credibility, not drama
• Move quickly when you have conviction.
• Use facts in diligence. Do not invent retrade reasons.
• A clean buyer with organized diligence often wins.
Step 8 — Close with margin
• Protect post-close liquidity.
• Do not stretch so hard that one surprise creates panic.
Step 9 — Run the first 100 days tightly
• Collections, inspections, work orders, tenant communication, turns, vendor control, reporting.
• Speed matters, but clarity matters more.
Step 10 — Turn deal one into deal two
• Document what actually happened versus the pro forma.
• Use the first asset as your operating education.

Recommended priority tiers
• Tier 1: Lakewood / West End, Walltown, Parkwood / South Durham
• Tier 2: Duke Park, Old East Durham
• Tier 3: Old West Durham, Cleveland-Holloway, Morehead Hill
• Selective only / not default first-deal hunting grounds: Old North Durham, Trinity Park
Neighborhood notes
Lakewood / West End
First-deal fit: 8.35 / 10
Tenant base: Young professionals, Duke-adjacent renters, service/medical workers, local-amenity seekers
Best deal type: 6–20 unit value-add, small portfolios, older vintage garden / walk-up
Why it works: Best balance of attainability, centrality, and renovation upside for a first-time buyer.
Main risk: Still competitive; do block-by-block underwriting.
Walltown
First-deal fit: 7.85 / 10
Tenant base: Duke-related workforce, grad students, workforce renters
Best deal type: Small multifamily and scattered small-balance rentals
Why it works: Lower basis than Trinity / Old West with strong Duke demand support.
Main risk: Perception and tenant quality can vary more by block.
Parkwood / South Durham
First-deal fit: 7.40 / 10
Tenant base: RTP workers, airport / logistics / healthcare workforce, suburban renter pool
Best deal type: 1980s–2000s value-add near RTP / Southpoint
Why it works: Best suburban first-deal option if you want depth of renter demand and easier management.
Main risk: Less neighborhood charm and more direct competition from newer product.
Old East Durham
First-deal fit: 7.30 / 10
Tenant base: Workforce renters, price-sensitive tenants, emerging creative/urban renter mix
Best deal type: Deep value-add, smaller buildings, patient hold
Why it works: Most upside and basis relief in the city.
Main risk: Highest execution risk; not ideal if you want a very easy first deal.
Duke Park
First-deal fit: 6.50 / 10
Tenant base: Urban renters wanting proximity to downtown and Duke
Best deal type: Small multifamily / house-scale rental hold
Why it works: Durable infill demand and strong long-term hold quality.
Main risk: Easy to overpay relative to first-deal economics.
Old West Durham
First-deal fit: 6.45 / 10
Tenant base: Duke-affiliated renters, higher-income urban renters
Best deal type: Selective infill hold / boutique multifamily

First-deal fit: 6.15 / 10

5. Deal-screening checklist
Before deep underwriting, ask:
• Is this one of my target neighborhoods?
• Do I understand exactly how NOI improves?
• Do I have a real T-12 and current rent roll?
• Are taxes, insurance, and capex believable?
• Is the tenant base stable enough for a first deal?
• Is the physical scope light enough that I can control it?
• Would I still want to own this if rent growth is flat?
• Am I attracted because the deal fits, or because I am scared to miss one?
Rule of thumb: if more than one hard gate fails, or if the asset only works under optimistic assumptions, pass for deal one.
6. How to use the buy box scorecard
• Fill only the blue cells in the workbook.
• Paste the neighborhood score from the deep-dive sheet.
• Rate qualitative factors honestly from 1 to 5.
• The sheet penalizes hard-gate failures even when the qualitative score looks decent.
• A score is not permission to buy. It is a forcing function for discipline.
Interpretation: PURSUE means the opportunity looks structurally suitable for a first deal. MAYBE means it needs stronger basis or cleaner diligence. PASS means it is probably not the right first acquisition.
7. Questions to ask your team
Broker
• Why is this asset really for sale?
• What sort of buyer should not buy this deal?
• What is the real obstacle to pushing rents or occupancy?
Property manager
• Could you take this over tomorrow without operational chaos?
• What will be hardest in the first 90 days?
• Where do you think the pro forma is too optimistic?
Lender
• What leverage and reserve level do you actually like here?
• What assumption are you least comfortable with?
Inspector / contractor
• What is the most likely hidden capital item?
• What would you fix first if this were your money?
Attorney
• Anything unusual in title, survey, zoning, permits, leases, or seller disclosures?
• What could delay close or create post-close regret?
